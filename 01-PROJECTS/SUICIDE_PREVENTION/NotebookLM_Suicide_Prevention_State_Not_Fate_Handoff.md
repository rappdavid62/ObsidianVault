## Core framing

The appendix should treat suicidality as a public-health, clinical, community, equity, and systems-design problem. It should avoid portraying suicide as a failure of willpower. Depression and suicidal thinking should be framed as states affected by risk stacks, protective factors, social connection, access to care, care transitions, acute stress, substance use, shame, isolation, and the surrounding environment.

State Not Fate should not claim to diagnose, assess, predict, or prevent suicide. It can support prevention-adjacent work by helping people learn, prepare to seek help, organize thoughts, reduce isolation, track protective routines, and connect to human support.

## Appendix map for NotebookLM to build

### 1. Why suicide prevention belongs in State Not Fate
- Depression can narrow perceived options.
- Suicidal thinking often grows through isolation, hopelessness, shame, acute stress, substance use, sleep disruption, and lack of reachable support.
- State Not Fate should frame suicidal states as serious, real, and changeable states that require human support, not personal failure.

### 2. Depression and suicidality
NotebookLM should gather sources on:
- Major depressive disorder and suicidal ideation.
- Anhedonia, hopelessness, agitation, insomnia, substance use, trauma, social isolation.
- Why most depressed people do not die by suicide, and why suicide risk should not be simplified to depression alone.
- How depression treatment, social support, and safety planning relate to suicide prevention.

### 3. Warning signs taxonomy
Build a non-graphic taxonomy:
- Baseline change: withdrawal, sleep change, increased substance use, hopelessness, burden statements.
- Escalation: direct/indirect death talk, trapped feeling, agitation, reckless behavior, giving away possessions, goodbye behavior.
- Acute concern: stated intent, plan, immediate access to lethal means, inability to stay safe, severe agitation or intoxication.

### 4. Risk stack / protection stack
Risk stack:
- Prior attempt
- Depression or other mental illness
- Acute loss
- Substance use
- Job/financial/legal crisis
- Isolation
- Hopelessness
- Chronic pain/serious illness
- Trauma
- Unsafe media exposure
- Easy access to lethal means
- Care transition/dropout

Protection stack:
- Reasons for living
- Connectedness
- Family/friend/community support
- Cultural identity
- Coping/problem-solving skills
- High-quality physical/behavioral healthcare
- Reduced access to lethal means
- Crisis resources
- Follow-up contact
- Belonging and purpose

### 5. Intervention map
Levels:
- Self: notice state narrowing; pause; contact support; use crisis plan.
- Friend/family: ask directly, listen, stay connected, involve support, reduce immediate danger.
- Peer supporter: boundaries, documentation, referral, warm handoff, nonjudgmental support.
- Clinician: assessment, safety planning, treatment, follow-up.
- Systems: 988, ED, primary care, schools, workplaces, postvention.
- Community: stigma reduction, outreach, trust-building, culturally grounded supports.

### 6. State Not Fate integration rules
The app/site can:
- Educate.
- Help users prepare to ask for help.
- Offer reflection prompts that encourage connection.
- Provide crisis-resource routing.
- Help users create a “talk to someone” script.
- Help users track protective routines.
- Provide non-clinical warning-sign education.
- Encourage safety planning with human support.

The app/site must not:
- Claim to assess, diagnose, predict, or prevent suicide.
- Provide risk scores.
- Ask for graphic method details.
- Present journaling as sufficient during crisis.
- Replace 988, emergency care, clinicians, or trusted human support.
- Create false certainty from checklists.

### 7. Public-facing language rules
Use:
- “died by suicide”
- “suicide death”
- “suicidal thoughts”
- “person in suicidal crisis”
- “support after suicide loss”
- “means safety” or “making the environment safer”

Avoid:
- “committed suicide”
- sensational details
- simple cause claims
- heroic/romantic framing
- graphic description
- location/method specificity
- shaming or blame language
- “attention-seeking” dismissal

### 8. Course/module ideas
Module 1: What suicidal thinking is and is not  
Module 2: Depression, narrowing, and state collapse  
Module 3: Warning signs and risk stacks  
Module 4: Protective factors and connection  
Module 5: How to talk to someone you are worried about  
Module 6: Safety planning and crisis resources  
Module 7: Aftercare, caring contacts, and follow-up  
Module 8: Postvention after a suicide death  
Module 9: Equity and culturally grounded prevention  
Module 10: State Not Fate boundaries and responsible design
