# Supporter Script Library: Practical Communication Guides

## Overview
This library provides practical scripts, language templates, and conversation guidelines for friends, family, peer supporters, and community members. 

*Goal: Reduce the friction of knowing what to say, preserve safety boundaries, and route effectively to professional care without inducing shame.*

---

## Part 1: Checking In (Friend / Family Member)

Use these scripts when you notice changes in a friend or family member's behavior (withdrawal, increased substance use, comments about feeling trapped or being a burden).

### Script A: Opening the Conversation (Low Pressure)
> "Hey [Name], I've noticed you've been a bit quieter than usual lately, and you mentioned feeling pretty exhausted last week. I care about you and just wanted to check in. How have things really been going?"

*If they respond with "I'm fine" but their behavior indicates otherwise:*
> "I hear you, and it's okay if you don't want to dive into it right now. I just want you to know I'm here if you ever want to talk, even if it's just to sit in silence or hang out without talking about heavy stuff."

### Script B: Asking Directly About Suicide
If you notice warning signs or a strong gut feeling, ask directly. **Do not beat around the bush.**
> "You've been carrying a massive amount of weight lately, and some of the things you've said make me worry. Sometimes when people are in this level of pain, they think about suicide. Are you having thoughts of suicide?"

---

## Part 2: Responding to Disclosures

### Script C: Responding to a "Yes" (I am thinking of suicide)
Your priority is validation and safety, not solving their problems.
> "Thank you for trusting me enough to tell me that. I know it takes a lot of strength to say it. I want you to know you are not alone, and I want to help you stay safe. Let's take a deep breath together. We don't have to solve your whole life right now—let's just focus on keeping you safe for the next hour. Do you have a crisis plan, or can we look at some resources together?"

*Bypassing immediate problem-solving:*
> "I know you're dealing with [relationship/job/money issues], and those are incredibly heavy. But right now, we don't need to fix those things. Let's just focus on keeping you safe right here, right now."

### Script D: Responding to a "No" (But they are still in deep distress)
> "I'm glad you're not having those thoughts, but I can see you're still in a really painful spot. You don't have to carry this alone. What's one small thing we can do today to make the environment feel a bit lighter? Can I bring you some food, or help you clean up, or just stay here with you?"

## Part 3: Establishing Boundaries (Peer Supporter)

As a peer supporter, you are an adjunct and a bridge, not a therapist. It is critical to set clear boundaries to protect your own mental health and ensure the user gets proper care.

### Script E: Defining Your Role (Early Interaction)
> "I'm really glad to walk with you through this. I want to be clear about my role: I'm here as a peer supporter to share tools, help you track routines, and listen when things get heavy. But I'm not a licensed therapist or a crisis counselor. If things ever reach a point where you feel unsafe or in danger, my boundary is that we will immediately bring in crisis professionals (like 988) or emergency services. Does that agreement make sense to you?"

### Script F: Addressing Drift or Crisis
If the user starts using you as a primary therapist or crisis intervention line:
> "I hear how much pain you're in, and I want to support you, but this is beyond what I can safely handle as a peer. I need to make sure you get the right support. Let's call or text 988 together right now, or call your therapist. I can stay on the line with you while we do it, but we need to take that step."

## Part 4: Postvention Check-Ins (Community / Workplace Leaders)

Postvention scripts support individuals after a suicide death has occurred in a community, workplace, or family.

### Script G: Workplace or Group Notice (Responsible Communication)
> "We are deeply saddened to share that our colleague/community member, [Name], died by suicide on [Date]. Our thoughts are with their family and loved ones. We know this loss is incredibly difficult and affects everyone differently. We want to support each other through this grief. We have arranged for support services to be available [provide details], and we encourage anyone who needs to talk or take time to do so. In U.S. crisis situations, please remember that 988 is available 24/7."

*What this script does right*:
- Names suicide directly without details (prevents rumors).
- Avoids graphic detail (method, location).
- Avoids romanticizing or listing single causes.
- Directs to support resources.

### Script H: Reaching Out to a Grieving Team Member
> "Hey [Name], I was so sorry to hear about [Deceased's Name]'s death. I know you two were close. There are no perfect words for this, but I wanted to check in. How are you holding up today? There is absolutely no pressure to work or respond to this quickly—your well-being is what matters most."

## Part 5: Non-Demanding Caring Contacts

Use these templates to follow up with someone who has recently experienced a crisis, hospital discharge, or transition. These require **no response** from the recipient.

### Template A: Standard Nondemanding Check-in
> "Hey [Name], just wanted to send a quick note to say I'm thinking of you today. No need to reply to this at all—just wanted you to know you're in my thoughts. Hope today has some gentle moments for you."

### Template B: Routine Support Check-in
> "Hi [Name]. I'm thinking about you and wishing you a quiet, restful week. No response needed. I'll check in again next week just to send some warmth."
