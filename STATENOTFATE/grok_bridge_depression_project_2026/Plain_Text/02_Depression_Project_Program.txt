Depression Project Program
Operating build | April 2026
1. Executive summary
The current program treats depression as an interaction between biology, psychology, behavior, environment, and repeated learning. The person does not just feel bad; they start to expect less reward, trust themselves less, avoid more, drift more, and read temporary states as permanent truth. That is why purely intellectual insight often fails to fix the problem.
The corrective strategy is practical. Reduce chaos. Restore load-bearing anchors. Build visible routines. Lower friction. Recreate believable evidence that effort can still matter. Sequence the work so early-stage systems are not asked to carry late-stage ambitions.
2. Design principles
Start with function. Measure what the illness is doing to sleep, work, hygiene, focus, follow-through, environment, social life, and ordinary life range.
Treat motivation as unstable. Use structure, cues, checklists, and tiers instead of waiting to feel ready.
Use leverage, not volume. Choose the few inputs that improve several other inputs at once.
Prefer repetition over drama. Repeated small signals retrain the system more reliably than temporary bursts of force.
Scale the plan to the actual battery. Do not use one standard for collapse days and strong days.
Separate state from identity. A depressive state can feel permanent while still being changeable.
3. Front-end sequence
This order matters. When a person feels that effort is pointless, giving them the right tools in the wrong order still fails. The front end has to restore usability before asking for larger execution.
4. Hope as the gatekeeper
In the current design, hope is not sentimental language. It is the working prediction that effort can still produce improvement. When that prediction collapses, the person may still understand the plan intellectually while refusing it behaviorally.
Low hope produces phrases like: what is the point, I already know this, nothing sticks, I cannot keep doing this, or small steps are fake.
The repair method is not arguing with the person. The repair method is generating credible, small, repeatable evidence.
The first operating wins should therefore be modest, visible, and hard to misread.
5. Core operating modules
A. Daily anchors
Daily anchors are the load-bearing substrate. They are boring on purpose because they have to survive weak days.
Wake time regularity
Morning light exposure
Hydration and simple meal timing
Medication consistency when prescribed
Minimum viable movement
Hygiene continuity
Brief environment reset
B. Behavior and execution
Minimum viable day
Low / medium / strong day rules
Visible planning
Friction reduction
Rescue protocol for collapse days
C. Cognitive and emotional regulation
Rumination interruption
Anti-self-attack counter-messaging
Thoughts treated as data, not verdicts
Short reflection instead of endless self-analysis
D. Environment and social structure
Reduce the hidden cost of action
Keep the room from becoming an accomplice to collapse
Preserve one or two reliable forms of contact
Prevent total social disappearance
E. Substance and coping reality
The program should not pretend numbing, avoidance, impulsive coping, or substance use are side notes. They change energy, judgment, routine stability, and follow-through cost. The system has to account for them honestly or the plan becomes fantasy.
F. Therapy, medication, and formal treatment
The project is not anti-treatment. Current formal guidance still supports psychological treatments, medication when indicated, shared decision-making, and regular review. The project treats those lanes as tools that should make the rest of the plan more usable, not as identity positions.
6. Daily execution architecture
The hierarchy prevents the all-or-nothing collapse. If Level 3 fails, the person still has Level 2. If Level 2 fails, Level 1 still protects tomorrow.
7. Launch rules
Pick a wake time you can actually defend.
Decide where morning light will happen.
Pick a movement floor small enough to survive weak days.
Write the minimum viable day and the rescue sequence.
Put basic supplies where action becomes easier: water, food, hygiene items, checklist, meds if prescribed.
Track one baseline week before overinterpreting the result.
8. Five-year sequence
9. Personalization logic
The questionnaire answers should shape the first message and first plan. Someone whose main issue is exhaustion needs a different opening than someone whose main issue is shame, self-attack, or hopelessness. Tone also matters. A useful message must be hearable, not merely correct.
Symptom tells you what is hitting function hardest.
Constraint tells you what makes execution expensive in real life.
Tone tells you how the person can still receive language.
Priority tells you what the intro needs to accomplish first.
Capacity tells you how large the first plan is allowed to be.
10. Failure logic
Most plans fail for predictable reasons: they are too large, too abstract, too mood-dependent, too optimized for strong days, too disconnected from the person’s constraints, or too ashamed to admit what the person is actually doing when they disappear.
11. Practical standard for success
Early success does not look cinematic. It looks like lower chaos, better mornings, fewer completely lost days, less interpretive whiplash, faster correction after bad hits, and a growing sense that tomorrow can still be shaped.
12. Evidence note
This build stays aligned with current formal depression guidance that supports psychological treatment, medication when indicated, shared decision-making, and ongoing reassessment. NICE’s adult depression guideline remains current and was last reviewed in January 2026, the VA/DoD major depressive disorder guideline remains the active 2022 framework on the VA site, NIMH continues to frame depression as a disorder affecting mood, body, and cognition, and WHO states that effective treatments include psychological treatments and medications, with antidepressants not needed for mild depression.
