Front-End Intake Guide
How to use questionnaire answers | April 2026
1. Read for the real bottleneck
Do not get distracted by eloquence. Look for what is actually breaking function.
If the person names several problems, prioritize the one that causes the most repeated damage.
Look especially for hopelessness, exhaustion, severe drift, self-attack, social disappearance, or chaos-heavy environment.
2. Decide what the introduction has to accomplish first
3. Match the tone
Clinical - use structure, clarity, and mechanism language.
Practical - emphasize small steps, logistics, and friction reduction.
Blunt - cut sentimentality and name predictable traps directly.
Emotionally real - name suffering, shame, disappointment, and misunderstanding without melodrama.
Hopeful without fake optimism - stress that change is difficult but not impossible, and explain why.
4. Size the first plan correctly
Use the stated capacity, not the helper’s wishful estimate. If the person says they have very little time or energy, build a survival-floor plan first. Oversized early plans destroy credibility.
5. Use hope as a checkpoint
Ask one quiet question: does this person still believe effort can change anything? If the answer is no or barely, the first task is not education overload. The first task is hope repair through believable outcomes.
6. Watch for complexity flags
Possible bipolar history, psychotic features, major substance destabilization, severe self-neglect, collapse of basic intake, or rapid deterioration should not be treated as a simple self-management case.
The project can still provide structure, but the helper should not confuse structure with full clinical coverage.
