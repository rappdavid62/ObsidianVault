Contents
How to use this manual
This section keeps the plan from being misunderstood before the year-by-year chapters begin.
A five-year depression plan is not a promise that life will unfold cleanly over exactly sixty months. It is a sequencing device. It prevents the common mistake of demanding late-stage outcomes from an early-stage nervous system. The five years are not arbitrary; they are a way of assigning the right job to the right phase.
The main error depressed people make with long plans is overreaching in the first strong week and then interpreting the crash as proof that nothing works. The main error helpers make is giving the same advice to someone who is barely functioning and someone who already has a reasonably stable base. This manual is designed to correct both mistakes.
Each year chapter is built around six questions: What is this year for? What has to be protected? What should not be chased yet? What does a normal good month look like? What tends to break the system at this stage? What does tracking need to capture so drift can be seen early?
The years are cumulative. Later years never replace the anchors that kept earlier years alive. Instead, later years sit on top of them. If the base layer collapses, later ambitions become self-punishment.
Use the worksheets in pencil or in recurring digital copies. They are meant to be used repeatedly, not admired once.
Score the system honestly but without drama. The point of tracking is earlier correction, not self-judgment.
If a year gets partially derailed, do not jump ahead to compensate. Tighten the current stage first.
When in doubt, return to the anchors that improve circadian regularity, nutrition timing, movement, environmental order, and reduction of rumination.
Five-year sequence at a glance
Year 1 - Stabilization and de-chaos
The job is not reinvention. The job is to stop the floor from moving.
What this year is for
Year 1 is the structural year. Depression has usually damaged rhythm, energy prediction, follow-through, self-trust, and the basic sense that a day can be directed. This year tries to reverse that damage by reducing chaos before chasing expansion. The plan should become visible, repeatable, and survivable on weak days.
The core wins in Year 1 are boring on purpose: a more stable wake time, more daylight exposure, fewer days without food or hydration structure, more consistent medication adherence if medication is part of the plan, better hygiene continuity, some movement most days, and a less hostile environment. These are not small wins. They are the substrate on which later years depend.
Year 1 should also reduce interpretation errors. Depression makes people read every bad day as evidence of total failure. The Year 1 system replaces this with a logistics model: low-energy day, medium-energy day, strong day, collapse day. That distinction matters because different states require different versions of the plan.
Anchor priorities for this year
Wake time regularity before perfect sleep quality.
Morning light exposure and a clean cue that the day has begun.
Hydration, simple meal timing, and fewer long underfed stretches.
Medication adherence if prescribed, with side-effect notes instead of vague resentment.
Minimum viable movement: walking, light strength, mobility, or gentle activity on most days.
Five- to ten-minute environmental resets that keep the space from becoming an accomplice to collapse.
Brief meditation, breathing, or mantra work used as interruption tools, not as mystical performances.
Written daily cards and weekly reviews so the plan exists outside mood.
Do not ask this year to do these jobs yet
Do not make Year 1 about ambitious social reinvention.
Do not make Year 1 about extreme physique goals or punishing exercise standards.
Do not add ten supplements, four apps, and a maximalist morning routine all at once.
Do not interpret partial stability as proof that the anchors can already be dropped.
Most common failure modes
Starting too large after a strong day, then collapsing and calling the plan fake.
Using insight as a substitute for action: thinking deeply about the plan without actually running it.
Confusing fatigue with laziness and then responding with self-attack rather than tiered execution.
Using days off to either fully disintegrate or massively overcorrect.
Keeping the environment so chaotic that every basic task has hidden friction.

Year 2 - Capacity building
The base exists. Now it has to carry more weight without breaking.
What this year is for
Year 2 is for turning fragile stability into usable capacity. The person is no longer only trying to prevent collapse; they are trying to expand how much life can be carried while remaining stable. That means better energy handling, more reliable task completion, stronger physical conditioning, and higher tolerance for ordinary demands.
The trap in Year 2 is premature triumphalism. Early gains can create the illusion that the whole disorder has been solved. Then the person quietly stops respecting sleep, rhythm, pacing, or recovery, and the system begins leaking again. Year 2 succeeds when the person gets stronger without becoming sloppy.
This is also a good year to improve the quality of core anchors: move from random walks to somewhat programmed exercise, from vague environment resets to actual systems, from bare medication compliance to more informed observation about benefit, side effects, and timing.
Anchor priorities for this year
Formalize exercise with a weekly pattern: walking, strength, yoga, mobility, or a hybrid.
Improve sleep protection: light timing, reduced late stimulation, better bedroom conditions, and insomnia countermeasures.
Use energy budgeting so strong days build momentum instead of creating a crash debt.
Increase nutrition consistency and reduce long reactive eating gaps.
Use slightly longer meditation or cognitive reorientation blocks where helpful.
Turn weekly review into actual calibration: what worked, what kept slipping, what needs simplification.
Do not ask this year to do these jobs yet
Do not treat better months as permission to become chronically sleep-deprived.
Do not fill every available slot with obligations just because energy is improved.
Do not chase identity repair entirely through productivity. That usually recreates brittleness.
Most common failure modes
Overspending energy on strong days and then resenting the system on weak days.
Letting work or one relationship consume the entire recovery budget.
Dropping basic trackers because they seem unnecessary right before the drift returns.
Adding too much intensity to exercise too fast and converting a mood tool into another failure arena.

Year 3 - Reintegration
The system should now support more life, not just less suffering.
What this year is for
Year 3 is where recovery becomes externally visible. The person is no longer focused only on symptom containment; they begin reconnecting with pleasure, interest, identity, and selected people. Reintegration means widening participation in life without sacrificing the anchors that made widening possible.
This phase often feels emotionally strange. Many people expect reintegration to feel triumphant. Instead it can feel awkward, delayed, and vaguely unreal. Depression may have narrowed life for so long that ordinary interest and contact feel unfamiliar before they feel natural. That is not failure. It is a normal lag between functioning and emotional re-enchantment.
Year 3 is also the right time to test more deliberate forms of meaning: hobbies, learning, selected friendships, more competent handling of work identity, and improved tolerance for ordinary social friction. The point is not to become hyper-social. The point is to stop building a life entirely around avoidance.
Anchor priorities for this year
Protect rhythm while adding social and identity experiments.
Use graded exposure to contact rather than all-or-nothing social performance.
Keep exercise and sleep steady so reintegration does not eat the base.
Add one meaning-bearing activity that is not just maintenance or work.
Keep review tools focused on both mood and participation: did life widen this month?
Do not ask this year to do these jobs yet
Do not interpret social awkwardness as proof that reintegration is impossible.
Do not blow up the quiet routines that stabilized you just to look more normal.
Do not chase constant novelty. Recovery is not measured by how busy you look.
Most common failure modes
Confusing re-entry discomfort with actual danger and retreating too fast.
Trying to rebuild identity all at once through romance, status, or dramatic self-narratives.
Dropping solitude quality and replacing it with low-grade overstimulation.
Mistaking boredom for a need for chaos.

Year 4 - Expansion without self-sabotage
This is where more ambition is allowed, but only if the base is still respected.
What this year is for
Year 4 is the expansion year. The person may now be capable of larger goals: more demanding work standards, better finances, deeper relationships, better physical condition, or more substantial projects. The danger is not ambition itself. The danger is using ambition to forget the laws of the nervous system.
Expansion should be selective. A depressed person who begins feeling better is often tempted to compensate for lost years by chasing everything at once. That impulse is understandable and destructive. Year 4 works when more challenge is added in a staged way and the base is actively defended rather than nostalgically remembered.
This year should also improve judgment about tradeoffs. Not every opportunity is worth the recovery cost. Some expansions nourish the life. Others simply reproduce overextension in a more glamorous form.
Anchor priorities for this year
Treat anchors as infrastructure, not beginner tools.
Match larger goals to recovery capacity instead of to ego urgency.
Schedule recovery on purpose when workload or social demand increases.
Use periodic deload weeks in exercise, work pressure, or social commitments.
Keep the review process honest enough to catch overreach early.
Do not ask this year to do these jobs yet
Do not confuse momentum with invulnerability.
Do not accept every role, relationship, or project that flatters the self-image.
Do not let long-term resentment build just because you can outwardly perform.
Most common failure modes
Recreating depression through chronic overcommitment rather than through passivity.
Using caffeine, adrenaline, or external pressure as substitutes for real stability.
Quietly sacrificing sleep and recovery because the external life looks successful.
Building a schedule that has no slack and then collapsing when life behaves like life.

Year 5 - Consolidation and selective ambition
The question is no longer 'Can I do more?' but 'What is worth sustaining?'
What this year is for
Year 5 is for consolidation. By now the person should be able to distinguish between activities that merely look like recovery and activities that actually support a good life. The task is to keep what is structurally healthy, remove what is ornamental or draining, and turn the whole system into something durable enough to survive future seasons of pressure.
A mature plan becomes more selective, not more maximalist. The person should know their non-negotiables, their early warning signs, the cost of certain tradeoffs, and the difference between expansion that deepens life and expansion that only flatters the ego. Year 5 is where self-trust becomes evidence-based.
This phase should also strengthen the person's ability to manage life transitions without wiping the slate. Moves, loss, conflict, health setbacks, burnout periods, or changing jobs should no longer erase the plan completely. They should trigger adaptation, not total amnesia.
Anchor priorities for this year
Clarify a permanent anchor set: the few practices you will keep even in hard years.
Refine workload, social life, and environment to match your actual nature and limits.
Identify which recovery supports are truly load-bearing and which are optional.
Turn maintenance reviews into seasonal strategy rather than endless crisis cleanup.
Use good periods to prepare for future rough periods instead of pretending they cannot return.
Do not ask this year to do these jobs yet
Do not abandon structure just because it no longer feels dramatic.
Do not assume that because life is bigger, old vulnerabilities no longer exist.
Do not keep carrying old systems that no longer fit, just because they once helped.
Most common failure modes
Letting boredom with maintenance become quiet self-sabotage.
Misreading ordinary sadness, stress, or grief as catastrophic relapse and destabilizing the system.
Forgetting to adapt the plan when life circumstances materially change.
Acting as if the plan should run itself forever without review.

Maintenance - Relapse prevention and durable functioning
This chapter answers what to do after the five years no longer feel like a project and start feeling like real life.
Maintenance is not the end of the plan. It is the adult form of the plan. The purpose of maintenance is to protect gains without turning life into an endless treatment ritual. In practice that means keeping a small, load-bearing set of anchors, continuing periodic review, and responding early to drift instead of waiting for collapse to provide clarity.
Relapse is often a process problem before it becomes a mood catastrophe. Sleep begins sliding. Meals get irregular. The environment gets sloppier. Exercise stops. Shame rises. Contact narrows. The internal narrative darkens. A good maintenance system is designed to catch these shifts while they are still correctable with tightening rather than full reconstruction.
The maintenance mindset is disciplined but not paranoid. The goal is not to scan for pathology all day. The goal is to remain close enough to the data of your own life that you know when the structure is loosening.
Maintenance rules
Keep a permanent anchor set that survives stress, grief, travel, and life transitions.
Review monthly even when life is going well; good months are when drift gets ignored.
Use an early-warning list with concrete behavioral markers, not just feelings.
Tighten early: more sleep protection, more light, simpler days, more food structure, less stimulation, less overcommitment.
Return to rescue protocol temporarily when needed without interpreting that as total regression.
Treat medication changes, supplement changes, major schedule changes, and major life changes as events that require extra monitoring.

The worksheet section is deliberately plain. It is supposed to be usable, not decorative. Use one page per month, quarter, or year as needed.

Source notes
These notes summarize the evidence used to sharpen the year structure and worksheet logic.
NICE depression guideline (reviewed January 2026): Used for the overall treatment architecture, relapse prevention emphasis, the value of exercise, mindfulness-based cognitive therapy, and the need to match intensity of intervention to severity and recurrence.
VA/DoD 2022 MDD guideline: Used for measurement-based care, PHQ-9-guided follow-up, continuation and maintenance logic, and the broader systems view of major depressive disorder management.
NIMH and WHO depression background material: Used to keep the front-end understanding of depression grounded in daily functioning, not just mood language.
2024 BMJ network meta-analysis on exercise for depression: Used to support exercise as a strong anchor; walking or jogging, yoga, and strength training were among the more effective modalities.
2024 meta-analysis of CBT-I in MDD with comorbid insomnia: Used to justify more aggressive attention to sleep and insomnia treatment because CBT-I improved depressive symptoms beyond the sleep domain.
2024 JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis on bright light therapy: Used to support bright light as an adjunct rather than as a magic standalone intervention.
Recent review-level evidence on omega-3, magnesium, and creatine: Used cautiously: omega-3 appears mixed but may help as an adjunct in some cases; magnesium shows possible benefit with limited quality evidence; creatine remains promising but uncertain and should be handled carefully, especially in bipolar disorder or kidney disease.
