
The Thing I Couldn’t See Through

It wasn’t a rainbow trip.

It wasn’t one of those little glowing mushroom-goblin bedtime stories people tell when they want psychedelics to sound like therapy with better lighting. This was not “the universe loves me.” This was not “everything is connected.” This was not a drum circle with cheekbones.

This was reality taking its skin off.

We were in the back of someone’s house, in one of those little glassed-in porch rooms that feel half indoors, half aquarium. TV on one side. Yard behind us. Neighbor’s house past the tree. My buddy sitting there like a normal human being, which lasted about three seconds.

Then he changed.

Not melted. Not blurred. Changed.

He compressed.

His body shortened and widened like something made of clay, brick, meat, and bad architecture. He looked packed down, squashed, hardened, like some invisible industrial press had decided human beings were inefficiently shaped. Then the surface of him stopped mattering. His skin became optional. His body turned transparent in layers.

I could see through him.

Not like X-ray vision. Not like a cartoon skeleton. More like he had become a rendered object, something built in slices. I could see him as anatomy and geometry at the same time. Kidney. Brain. Bone. Tissue. Little cross-sections. Little planes. Like a 3D printer running in reverse. Like a videogame model with the texture removed. Like I had suddenly become a four-dimensional thing looking down at a three-dimensional thing trying to pretend it was solid.

And then he stopped being the most impossible part.

Behind him was the tree.

The tree disappeared, then began again.

I watched it start as a seed. I watched the dirt hold it. I watched it push up into a sapling while the house behind it rewound into older versions of itself. Boards changed. Windows changed. The yard changed. The whole scene moved backward and forward at once, like time had become a stack of glass plates and I could slide my eyes through each one.

The tree got taller. The house got built, rebuilt, remodeled, aged, sharpened, modernized. The branches grew into themselves. The future came through in flashes — angular, strange, too clean, like somebody had replaced the neighborhood with a machine’s idea of a neighborhood. Then the tree kept going. Past us. Past the people in the house. Past the version of the world I belonged to. I saw it live out more time than I knew how to hold.

A hundred years. Maybe more. Maybe less. Time was not presenting credentials.

I could see through the yard.

I could see through the house.

I could see through the neighbor’s walls.

I could see people inside, arranged in space like furniture with souls.

And then I looked at the TV.

Nothing.

That was the part that bothered me.

Not the transparent bodies. Not the time-lapse tree. Not the house aging backward and forward like a haunted blueprint. The TV bothered me because it refused me.

I could see through a human body. I could see through brick. I could see through wood, glass, distance, and apparently time itself.

But I could not see through the television.

The stupid glowing box just sat there, playing some ordinary garbage, holding its surface like a locked door. Reality had become negotiable. Flesh had become negotiable. Architecture had become negotiable. The past and future had become negotiable.

But the screen stayed sovereign.

That is the part I still cannot explain. Not cleanly. Not without sounding insane or fake-profound. The TV was the one object in the room that acted like it had more authority than matter.

Later, after I came down enough to move around like a person with a name, I went over and knocked on the neighbor’s door.

There were four people inside.

Three of them were exactly where I had seen them.

The fourth answered the door.

So either my brain built the most convincing hallucination I have ever had, down to the placement of strangers behind a wall, or something happened that does not fit inside the little cardboard filing cabinet we call sanity.

I am not saying I proved anything.

I am saying that, for a few minutes, I saw the world as editable. Flesh, time, houses, trees — all of it became layers. The only thing that would not open was the screen.

And maybe that is the real horror.

Not that drugs can make you see things.

That the machine was the one thing I couldn’t see through.
